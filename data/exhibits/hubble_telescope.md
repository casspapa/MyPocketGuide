## Overview
This full-scale replica of the Hubble Space Telescope represents one of the most scientifically productive instruments ever built. Launched in 1990 and still operational, Hubble has revolutionised astronomy — from measuring the age of the universe to discovering that its expansion is accelerating, a finding that led to the Nobel Prize in Physics in 2011.

## Key Facts
- Launched: April 24, 1990 aboard Space Shuttle Discovery
- Orbit: 547km above Earth's surface
- Orbital period: approximately 95 minutes
- Mirror diameter: 2.4 metres
- Length: 13.2 metres, diameter: 4.2 metres
- Weight: approximately 11,000kg
- Has made over 1.5 million observations of more than 50,000 celestial objects
- Generated over 100 terabytes of data
- Serviced five times by Space Shuttle crews (1993, 1997, 1999, 2002, 2009)
- The 1993 servicing mission corrected a flaw in the primary mirror ground to the wrong shape by 2.2 micrometres
- Powers itself via two solar panels generating about 2,800 watts
- Successor (James Webb Space Telescope) launched December 25, 2021

## Scientific Significance
Hubble's contributions to astronomy are without parallel in the modern era. The Hubble Deep Field images (1995, 1996) — long exposures of apparently empty patches of sky — revealed thousands of galaxies and transformed understanding of the universe's structure and history. Hubble measurements of Cepheid variable stars in distant galaxies provided the first precise measurement of the Hubble Constant — the rate of the universe's expansion. The unexpected discovery that this expansion is accelerating (implying the existence of "dark energy") was made using Hubble supernova observations and earned the 2011 Nobel Prize. Hubble also characterised the atmospheres of exoplanets and directly imaged the collision of Comet Shoemaker-Levy 9 with Jupiter in 1994.

## History & Controversy
Hubble's launch was a disaster — the primary mirror had been ground to the wrong shape due to a calibration error, producing blurry images. NASA faced intense public ridicule. The 1993 servicing mission, which installed corrective optics (described as "contact lenses for Hubble"), is considered one of the greatest triumphs of human spaceflight — astronauts conducted five spacewalks to restore the telescope's vision. The mission transformed NASA's public image. Hubble was nearly decommissioned in 2004 when NASA cancelled its final servicing mission following the Columbia disaster. Public and scientific outcry led to the decision being reversed, and a 2009 mission extended Hubble's operational life significantly.

## For Young Visitors
Hubble is a giant camera floating in space that takes pictures of things billions of light years away! When it was launched it had blurry vision — like needing glasses — so astronauts flew up in a space shuttle and fixed it while floating in space. Some of Hubble's pictures show galaxies so far away that the light we're seeing left them before Earth even existed. It's still working today, more than 30 years later, which is much longer than anyone expected!

## Conservation Status
The actual Hubble Space Telescope remains in orbit and is still operational as of 2026, though in a reduced capacity with three gyroscopes remaining. NASA and ESA continue to operate it. The telescope is not expected to be retrieved — when it eventually re-enters Earth's atmosphere, it will largely burn up. The replica in this gallery was constructed from original engineering drawings and is maintained to museum exhibition standard.
