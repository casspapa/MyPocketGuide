## Visual Identification

Commonly called: "Dippy", "the dinosaur", "the diplodocus", "the long-necked dinosaur"
Visual cues: 26-metre plaster cast dinosaur skeleton with extremely long neck and whip-like tail. Four-legged stance, relatively small skull. Off-white colour. Takes up most of the floor space.

## Overview
Dippy is a plaster cast of a Diplodocus carnegii skeleton, one of the most beloved dinosaur exhibits in the world. For 112 years Dippy stood in the central hall of the Natural History Museum London before being displaced by Hope the Blue Whale in 2017. Dippy is named after Andrew Carnegie, the American steel magnate who funded the original excavation.

## Key Facts
- 26 metres long — longer than Hope the Blue Whale
- Cast made from bones excavated in Wyoming, USA in 1898
- Original bones are in the Carnegie Museum of Natural History, Pittsburgh
- Diplodocus lived approximately 154-152 million years ago (Late Jurassic)
- Herbivore — fed on low-lying vegetation using its long neck
- Tail had up to 80 vertebrae and was used as a whip-like counterbalance
- Brain was roughly the size of a fist despite its enormous body
- Carnegie had 9 identical casts made and gifted them to museums worldwide

## Scientific Significance
Diplodocus carnegii is one of the most completely known sauropod dinosaurs. Its discovery in the 1890s transformed scientific understanding of how large dinosaurs moved — early theories that it dragged its tail were overturned in the 1990s when biomechanical analysis confirmed it held its tail horizontally as a counterbalance to its neck. The species name carnegii honours Andrew Carnegie directly.

## History & Controversy
Carnegie gifted a cast to King Edward VII in 1905 — the king reportedly saw a sketch and demanded one for the nation. The original casting process took 18 months. When Hope the Blue Whale replaced Dippy in Hintze Hall in 2017, the public outcry was immediate — a petition to keep Dippy attracted hundreds of thousands of signatures. The NHM responded by sending Dippy on a UK-wide tour, visiting eight venues across Britain. Dippy's displacement was a deliberate curatorial statement about living conservation over extinct species.

## For Young Visitors
Dippy is so long that if you lay him down outside, he'd stretch almost to the end of a football pitch! He ate nothing but plants his whole life, using his incredibly long neck to reach leaves. Despite being huge, his brain was only about as big as your fist. Scientists used to think he dragged his tail, but it turns out he held it up in the air like a massive whip!

## Conservation Status
As a plaster cast, Dippy requires periodic restoration rather than traditional conservation. The cast was painstakingly cleaned and restored before its UK tour. The original Wyoming bones from which Dippy was cast are preserved in Pittsburgh under strict environmental controls.
