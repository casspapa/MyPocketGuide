# Caryatid of the Erechtheion

## Visual Identification
Keywords: marble female figure, draped robes, architectural column, Greek sculpture, basket on head
Commonly called: "the caryatid", "the Greek woman", "the column woman", "the marble lady"
Visual cues: 2.3-metre marble sculpture of a standing woman in flowing draped robes. Carries a basket (kalathos) on her head. One leg bears weight with thick vertical drapery folds. White Pentelic marble, classical Greek style.

## Overview
This caryatid is one of the original six sculpted female figures that served as architectural columns on the south porch of the Erechtheion temple on the Acropolis of Athens. Dating to approximately 421-406 BCE, she is among the most iconic sculptures of the ancient world. Five of the six originals are in Athens; one — this figure — has been in the British Museum since 1801.

## Key Facts
- Carved approximately 421-406 BCE during the Classical period of ancient Greece
- Height: 2.3 metres including base
- Made from Pentelic marble quarried from Mount Pentelicus near Athens
- One of six caryatids that supported the roof of the Erechtheion's south porch
- Five originals remain in Athens at the Acropolis Museum
- Removed by Lord Elgin in 1801 and sold to the British Museum in 1816
- The Erechtheion was built on the most sacred spot of the Acropolis — where Athena and Poseidon competed for the city
- "Caryatid" derives from Karyai, a town whose women allegedly danced at religious festivals

## Scientific Significance
The caryatids represent a pinnacle of Greek sculptural achievement in solving a structural engineering problem with artistic elegance. The figures bear the weight of the porch roof through their heads via a carved basket (kalathos). The drapery of their robes was deliberately carved in thick vertical folds on the weight-bearing leg, mimicking the fluting of a traditional column while disguising the structural function. Modern analysis using X-ray fluorescence has revealed traces of original paint — the marble was never intended to be white.

## History & Controversy
The caryatid's removal is one of the most contested acts in the history of cultural heritage. Lord Elgin, British Ambassador to the Ottoman Empire, obtained a permit from Ottoman authorities to remove architectural elements from the Acropolis. Greece has formally requested the return of the caryatid and the Parthenon Marbles since 1983. The British Museum's position is that the sculptures are part of world heritage best preserved and displayed in London. The Greek government's position is that separating the caryatid from her sisters is an act of cultural violence — in Athens, the remaining five caryatids appear to lean toward the gap left by her absence.

## For Young Visitors
Imagine a building where instead of boring round pillars holding up the roof, there are six women carved from marble! That's exactly what the ancient Greeks built. This woman has been standing for over 2,400 years. She was taken from Greece over 200 years ago and Greece still wants her back — it's one of the biggest arguments in the art world today.

## Conservation Status
The caryatid in Athens were moved indoors to the Acropolis Museum in 1979 to protect them from acid rain and air pollution. The British Museum specimen underwent major conservation in the 1930s when iron clamps inserted in the 19th century were found to be rusting and expanding, cracking the marble. These were replaced with titanium. Surface cleaning removed 19th century over-painting.
