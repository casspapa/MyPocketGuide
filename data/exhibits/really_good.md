## Overview
Really Good is a 7-metre-high bronze sculpture of a hand giving a thumbs up sign, with a disproportionately elongated thumb, created by British artist David Shrigley for the Mayor of London's Fourth Plinth Programme in Trafalgar Square. Unveiled on September 29, 2016, the work occupied the plinth until early 2018. Cast in bronze with a dark patina to match the historical statues surrounding it in the square, Really Good is both a satirical and sincere gesture — Shrigley's ambition was that it would become a self-fulfilling prophecy, that things considered 'bad', such as the economy, the weather and society, would benefit from a change of consensus towards optimism.

## Key Facts
- Artist: David Shrigley (born 1968, Macclesfield, England)
- Date: 2016 (unveiled September 29, 2016; remained until early 2018)
- Medium: bronze with dark patina
- Dimensions: 680 × 380 × 160cm
- Original location: Fourth Plinth, Trafalgar Square, London
- Commission: Mayor of London's Fourth Plinth Programme
- The tallest sculpture ever displayed on the Fourth Plinth at the time of its unveiling
- The Fourth Plinth was designed by Sir Charles Barry and built in 1841 for an equestrian statue of William IV that was never completed due to insufficient funds
- The dark patina was deliberately chosen to match the surrounding 19th century statues of military heroes and royalty in Trafalgar Square
- The thumb is deliberately elongated beyond anatomical proportion — the "comic extension" brings the total height to the full 7 metres
- Shrigley was nominated for the Turner Prize in 2013 for his Hayward Gallery exhibition Brain Activity
- The original bronze was permanently acquired by the National Gallery of Victoria, Melbourne in 2024 through the Felton Bequest
- The Museum displays an authorised replica; the original is at NGV International, Melbourne

## Scientific Significance
The sculpture was cast in bronze using traditional techniques adapted for monumental scale. The dark patina was applied through chemical surface treatment of the bronze to produce a finish visually consistent with the 19th century statuary in Trafalgar Square. The deliberate anatomical distortion of the thumb — exaggerated well beyond natural proportion — places the work in a long tradition of monumental figurative sculpture that uses proportional distortion to convey meaning, from ancient Egyptian colossi to Claes Oldenburg's oversized everyday objects. Where those precedents typically amplified power or monumentality, Shrigley's distortion amplifies a banal, everyday gesture to absurd scale, inverting the convention.

## History & Controversy
Really Good was unveiled in September 2016, months after the Brexit referendum that divided Britain. The timing was central to the work's reception — while London Mayor Sadiq Khan praised it as capturing the city's spirit of optimism, positivity and inclusivity, many interpreted it as a sardonic thumbs up to Brexit Britain. Shrigley described the work as being about "making the world a better place… which obviously is a ridiculous proposition, but I think it's a good proposition." The sculpture divided critics: the Guardian's Jonathan Jones dismissed it, while others praised its democratic accessibility — a universal gesture readable across languages and cultures. It became one of the most photographed and socially shared Fourth Plinth commissions.

The Fourth Plinth itself stood empty for over 150 years before the contemporary art programme began. Built in 1841 for an equestrian statue of William IV that was never funded, it became a site for temporary commissions from 1999 onward, featuring works by Marc Quinn, Yinka Shonibare, Katharina Fritsch, and Hans Haacke. Really Good remained on the plinth until early 2018.

In 2023, the original bronze travelled from the UK to Melbourne for the NGV Triennial, where it was displayed on the forecourt of NGV International from December 2023. In March 2024, the NGV announced the permanent acquisition of the sculpture through the Felton Bequest — one of Australia's most significant charitable art funds, established in 1904. Shrigley has a long relationship with the NGV, which presented his first major Australian survey exhibition in 2014.

## For Young Visitors
Imagine a giant thumbs up — taller than a house — sitting right in the middle of one of London's most famous squares, surrounded by serious statues of kings and military heroes! Artist David Shrigley made this enormous bronze hand because he thought the world needed cheering up. The thumb is deliberately too long, which makes it look a bit funny and a bit strange. Shrigley hoped that if enough people saw a giant thumbs up every day, they might start feeling more positive about things. The empty plinth it stood on waited over 150 years for a statue — and this is what it got! The sculpture now lives in Melbourne, Australia, where it greets visitors outside the National Gallery of Victoria.

## Conservation Status
The original bronze was removed from the Fourth Plinth in early 2018 and subsequently acquired by the National Gallery of Victoria, Melbourne, where it is displayed on the forecourt of NGV International. Bronze sculptures with applied patina require monitoring for bronze disease — cyclical corrosion caused by chloride contamination — particularly after extended outdoor exposure. The Museum displays an authorised scale replica. The replica is maintained under standard indoor museum conditions with controlled humidity to preserve the patina finish.
