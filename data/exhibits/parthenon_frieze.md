## Overview
The Parthenon Frieze is a continuous sculptural band that originally ran around the upper interior of the Parthenon temple on the Acropolis of Athens, depicting the Panathenaic Procession — Athens' greatest religious festival. Carved between 447-432 BCE under the direction of the sculptor Pheidias, it represents the high point of Classical Greek relief sculpture. Approximately half of the surviving frieze is in the British Museum; most of the remainder is in Athens.

## Key Facts
- Carved 447-432 BCE under the supervision of Pheidias
- Original length: 160 metres running around all four sides of the Parthenon interior
- Height of frieze: approximately 1 metre
- Depicts approximately 378 figures — human, equestrian, and divine
- Carved in low relief from Pentelic marble
- About 50% survives — split between the British Museum and Athens
- Removed by Lord Elgin between 1801-1812
- Subject of the frieze debated for centuries — now generally accepted as the Panathenaic Procession held every four years
- Originally brightly painted and adorned with bronze accessories (reins, weapons) inserted into carved holes

## Scientific Significance
The Parthenon Frieze is a masterclass in the illusionistic techniques of Classical Greek sculpture. The relief is deliberately carved more deeply at the top than the bottom to compensate for the upward viewing angle — a sophisticated understanding of perspective that would not be matched in Western art for nearly two millennia. Recent photogrammetric analysis has created complete 3D digital reconstructions allowing scholars to study the original positioning for the first time. Pigment analysis using reflectance transformation imaging has revealed a vivid colour scheme of reds, blues, and golds.

## History & Controversy
The frieze's removal by Lord Elgin is the central controversy of cultural heritage law. The Parthenon was damaged by a Venetian artillery bombardment in 1687 when Ottoman forces stored gunpowder inside it — the explosion destroyed the central section of the building. Elgin's agents used saws to cut off sections of the frieze, in the process damaging some sculptures. The Greek government's campaign for reunification began formally in 1983 under Culture Minister Melina Mercouri. In 2023, the British Museum opened confidential reunification talks — the first time such discussions had been formally acknowledged.

## For Young Visitors
Imagine carving a picture story in stone that goes all the way around a building the size of a football field — that's what ancient Greek sculptors did! The frieze shows a huge parade with horses, chariots, and people bringing gifts to the goddess Athena. It was carved nearly 2,500 years ago and was originally painted in bright colours with real bronze weapons added on. Today half of it is in London and half is in Athens, and there's a big argument about whether it should be put back together.

## Conservation Status
The British Museum specimens are displayed in the purpose-built Duveen Gallery. A controversial cleaning in the 1930s used copper chisels and carborundum to scrub the surface, removing the original patina. This was done at the request of Lord Duveen who preferred white marble. The damage was only acknowledged publicly by the British Museum in 1999. The Athens sections are displayed in the Acropolis Museum, purpose-built in 2009 with a dedicated Parthenon gallery where the gaps left by the London pieces are clearly visible.
