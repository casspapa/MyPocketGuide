## Overview
The Mona Lisa (La Gioconda) is the most famous painting in the world. Painted by Leonardo da Vinci between approximately 1503-1519, it depicts Lisa Gherardini, wife of Florentine merchant Francesco del Giocondo. The original hangs in the Louvre in Paris behind bulletproof glass, visited by approximately 10 million people per year. The Museum displays a museum-quality authorised reproduction alongside the extraordinary scientific story of the original.

## Key Facts
- Artist: Leonardo da Vinci (1452-1519)
- Painted: approximately 1503-1519
- Medium: oil on poplar wood panel
- Dimensions: 77cm × 53cm — smaller than most visitors expect
- Subject: most likely Lisa Gherardini (1479-1542), wife of Francesco del Giocondo
- Location of original: Louvre Museum, Paris — Room 711
- Acquisition by France: purchased by King Francis I of France, approximately 1516-1519
- Stolen from the Louvre: August 21, 1911 by Vincenzo Peruggia — recovered 1913
- Attacked twice: 1956 (acid), 1974 (red spray paint), 2009 (tea cup), 2022 (cake)
- Estimated value: priceless — effectively uninsurable; last formal valuation in 1962: $100 million ($1 billion+ today)

## Scientific Significance
The Mona Lisa is a landmark in the history of painting technique. Leonardo pioneered sfumato — a technique of applying thin, semi-transparent layers of paint to create imperceptible transitions between light and shadow that give the face its lifelike quality. The famous enigmatic smile exploits the human eye's peripheral vision — the smile appears more pronounced when you look at her eyes than when you look directly at her mouth, because peripheral vision is more sensitive to low spatial frequencies. X-ray and infrared reflectography have revealed three distinct underpaintings beneath the final surface, showing Leonardo substantially revised the composition multiple times.

## History & Controversy
The 1911 theft by Vincenzo Peruggia — a Louvre employee who hid in a cupboard overnight — made the painting world-famous. Before the theft, the Mona Lisa was known to art historians but not the general public. Peruggia believed he was repatriating a stolen Italian masterpiece to Italy; in fact Leonardo had brought it to France himself. The painting was missing for two years and was displayed for weeks in Florence after its recovery, drawing massive crowds. The identity of the sitter was debated for centuries — the Lisa Gherardini identification is now supported by a 2005 discovery of a margin note in a contemporary document by Agostino Vespucci. In 2021, a submerged landscape painted as the background was identified as the Valdarno valley in Tuscany.

## For Young Visitors
The Mona Lisa is smaller than an iPad — most people are shocked when they see how tiny it is! For 400 years nobody outside the art world knew it existed, then someone stole it from the Louvre and suddenly everyone wanted to see what the fuss was about. The thief hid inside the museum overnight, walked out with it under his coat, and kept it under his bed for two years. Her smile is a kind of optical illusion — it looks different depending on where you're looking on her face. Scientists have found three different earlier versions of the painting hidden underneath.

## Conservation Status
The original Mona Lisa is in a fragile condition — the poplar wood panel has developed a small crack at the top and the paint layer shows fine craquelure (a network of cracks). The painting is kept at a constant 18-21°C and 50-55% relative humidity in a specially built climate-controlled case. It has not been cleaned or restored since 1809. Recent proposals to move it to a dedicated room to reduce overcrowding are under active consideration by the Louvre.
