# Hope the Blue Whale

**Exhibit ID:** hope_blue_whale
**Gallery:** Echoes of the Deep
**Location:** [120, 340]
**Connected Exhibits:** dippy_diplodocus, coelacanth

## Visual Identification
Keywords: whale skeleton, suspended ceiling, bones, massive, Hintze Hall
Commonly called: "Hope", "the whale", "blue whale", "the whale skeleton", "the big skeleton on the ceiling"
Visual cues: Enormous 25.2-metre whale skeleton suspended from the ceiling. Long jawbones, visible ribcage, vertebral column stretching the full length. Cream/white bones. Dominates the entire hall.


## Key Facts
- Scientific name: Balaenoptera musculus
- 25.2 metres in length, suspended from the ceiling of Hintze Hall
- Weighs approximately 10 tonnes as a skeleton
- Heart weighs approximately 180kg — roughly the size of a small car
- Blood vessels wide enough for a human to crawl through
- Largest animal species ever to have lived on Earth
- Blue whales can reach speeds of 50km/h and dive to 500 metres
- A blue whale call can reach 188 decibels — louder than a jet engine

## History & Provenance
- Found stranded on the coast of Wexford, Ireland, in March 1891
- Acquired by the Natural History Museum London shortly after stranding
- Originally displayed lying on the floor of Hintze Hall for over 100 years
- Re-suspended from the ceiling in 2017 after a major 3-year conservation project
- Named Hope in 2017 as a symbol of humanity's responsibility to the environment
- The naming was a deliberate statement about conservation and the future of the planet

## Scientific Context
- Blue whales are the largest animals ever known to have existed on Earth
- Despite their size they feed almost exclusively on krill — tiny shrimp-like crustaceans
- A blue whale can eat up to 4 tonnes of krill per day during feeding season
- They communicate using complex low-frequency vocalisations across vast ocean distances
- Blue whale populations were devastated by commercial whaling in the 20th century
- Estimated only 10,000-25,000 blue whales remain in the wild today
- Listed as Endangered on the IUCN Red List
- Recovery has been slow despite international whaling bans since 1966

## Controversies & Interesting Angles
- The decision to name her Hope was controversial — some felt it was too political for a museum
- The 2017 reinstallation displaced Dippy the Diplodocus who had stood in the hall since 1979
- Dippy's removal caused public outcry and a national petition in the UK
- The choice of a whale over a dinosaur was framed as prioritising living conservation over extinct species
- Hope's original stranding cause is still debated — possibly illness, disorientation, or injury

## Connections to Other Exhibits
- Dippy the Diplodocus was displaced by Hope's reinstallation — a deliberate curatorial statement
- The Coelacanth in this gallery represents another species once thought extinct or near-extinction
- Together the three exhibits in Echoes of the Deep span geological time from prehistoric to modern
