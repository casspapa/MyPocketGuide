## Overview
This full-scale reconstruction of a Megalodon jaw is based on the most complete fossil evidence available, primarily from teeth found globally. Otodus megalodon was the largest predatory fish ever to have lived — a shark that reached an estimated 15-20 metres in length and preyed on large whales. It went extinct approximately 3.6 million years ago.

## Key Facts
- Species: Otodus megalodon (formerly Carcharodon megalodon)
- Estimated length: 15-20 metres (conservative estimates 14-15m, some suggest up to 20m)
- Estimated weight: 50-100 tonnes
- Jaw reconstruction diameter: approximately 3 metres
- Individual teeth up to 18cm in length
- Bite force estimated at 108,514-182,201 newtons — the most powerful bite of any known animal
- Fed primarily on large baleen whales
- Existed from approximately 23 million years ago to 3.6 million years ago
- Teeth are the primary fossil evidence — cartilaginous skeleton rarely fossilises
- Found on every continent except Antarctica

## Scientific Significance
Megalodon body size estimation is based almost entirely on teeth, using the relationship between tooth size and body length established from living great white sharks (its closest relative, Carcharodon carcharias). The 2022 study by Shimada et al. used vertebral centra fossils from a specimen in the Royal Belgian Institute of Natural Sciences to estimate a 9.2 metre juvenile — consistent with adult estimates of 14-15 metres. The extinction of megalodon approximately 3.6 million years ago correlates with a decline in large whale populations (their primary prey) during a global cooling event, and potentially the emergence of competing apex predators including early great white sharks and killer whales.

## History & Controversy
The claim that megalodon still exists in the deep ocean is among the most persistent cryptozoological myths. It is comprehensively false — megalodon was a coastal and open-ocean species that fed near the surface where large whale populations concentrated. If it existed today its hunting activity and teeth would be continually discovered. The myth was amplified by the 2013 Discovery Channel documentary "Megalodon: The Monster Shark Lives" — a fictional mockumentary presented as fact, which Discovery Channel has since acknowledged was irresponsible. The reclassification from Carcharodon to Otodus in recent years reflects genetic and morphological evidence that megalodon is more closely related to a different lineage than the great white.

## For Young Visitors
Megalodon was a shark so big that a great white shark — already the biggest predatory fish alive today — could swim inside its mouth! It hunted whales the same way a lion hunts zebras. Its teeth are so big and heavy that they sink to the ocean floor and get preserved as fossils. People sometimes find them on beaches. Megalodon went extinct about 3.6 million years ago — long before humans existed — so don't worry, it's definitely not still out there!

## Conservation Status
Otodus megalodon is extinct. Fossil teeth are found globally and are relatively common compared to most large vertebrate fossils. Commercial fossil collection has raised concerns about scientific specimens being sold before study. Several countries have introduced regulations protecting megalodon fossil sites on public lands.
