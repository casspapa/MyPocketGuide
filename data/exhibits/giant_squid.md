## Overview
The giant squid (Architeuthis dux) is the largest invertebrate on Earth and one of the most mysterious creatures in the ocean. For centuries it existed only in sailor mythology and partial remains — a complete live specimen was not filmed in its natural habitat until 2004. The Museum's specimen is preserved in a 9-metre tank of formalin solution, one of the finest examples in the world.

## Key Facts
- Species: Architeuthis dux — the sole species in genus Architeuthis
- Maximum confirmed length: 13 metres (including tentacles)
- Maximum confirmed weight: approximately 275kg
- Eyes up to 30cm in diameter — the largest eyes of any living animal
- Has eight arms and two longer feeding tentacles lined with suckers ringed with sharp teeth
- Lives at depths of 300-1,000 metres in all the world's oceans
- Brain is donut-shaped — the oesophagus passes through the centre of the brain
- Uses jet propulsion and fins for locomotion
- Bioluminescent — can produce light in the deep ocean
- First live footage in natural habitat: 2004, off the Ogasawara Islands, Japan
- First video of a live specimen in its natural habitat: 2012

## Scientific Significance
Giant squids are the primary prey of sperm whales. The sucker scars found on sperm whale skin provided indirect evidence of their existence and size for over a century before live specimens were observed. Architeuthis dux has a single species classification that is genetically surprisingly uniform worldwide — suggesting the animals travel vast distances and interbreed across oceans. Their nervous system, despite being invertebrate, shows remarkable complexity. The giant axons of their nervous system — among the largest nerve fibres of any animal — were instrumental in early neuroscience research into how nerve signals propagate.

## History & Controversy
Giant squids were long dismissed as sea monster mythology — the Norse Kraken legend is almost certainly based on encounters with giant or colossal squids. Jules Verne's 20,000 Leagues Under the Sea (1870) featured a giant squid attack based on real accounts. The first confirmed specimen was found beached in Newfoundland in 1878. Securing an intact specimen for museum display was a decades-long pursuit — most washed ashore already decomposed. The Natural History Museum London's specimen required specialist preservation techniques developed specifically for this purpose. The question of maximum size remains open — unconfirmed reports of specimens up to 20 metres persist.

## For Young Visitors
The giant squid is real — and it's basically a sea monster! Its eyes are as big as footballs, the biggest eyes of any animal on Earth. It lives so deep in the ocean that scientists didn't get their first video of a live one until 2012. Sperm whales hunt and eat them — you can tell because sperm whales often have big circular scars from the squid's teeth-lined suckers. The squid's brain is shaped like a donut with its throat going through the hole in the middle!

## Conservation Status
Giant squids are not listed on the IUCN Red List due to insufficient population data. Preservation of museum specimens is challenging — the standard formalin-glycerol method requires periodic maintenance and the specimens continue to degrade over decades. The NHM London specimen required re-preservation in 2017. Deep-sea species like Architeuthis are difficult to study and their population status is essentially unknown.
