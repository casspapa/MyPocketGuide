## Overview
The Willamette Meteorite is the largest meteorite ever found in North America and the sixth largest in the world. Weighing 15.5 tonnes and measuring 2.1 metres tall, it is an iron-nickel meteorite that fell to Earth approximately 1 billion years ago. It holds deep spiritual significance for the Clackamas people of the Confederated Tribes of Grand Ronde, who called it Tomanowos — Heavenly Visitor.

## Key Facts
- Weight: 15.5 tonnes
- Dimensions: 2.1 metres tall, 1.4 metres wide
- Composition: iron-nickel alloy (91% iron, 7.62% nickel)
- Classification: coarse octahedrite — one of the rarest meteorite types
- Fell to Earth approximately 1 billion years ago
- Originally landed in what is now Canada or Montana, transported to Oregon by glaciers during the last Ice Age
- Discovered in 1902 by farmer Ellis Hughes in Clackamas County, Oregon
- Hughes secretly moved it 1km to his property over three months to claim it — later ruled theft
- Purchased by Mrs. William Dodge and donated to the American Museum of Natural History in 1906
- The Museum holds a precision cast; the original is in New York

## Scientific Significance
The Willamette meteorite formed in the core of a planetesimal — a small proto-planet — that was destroyed in a collision in the early solar system. Its crystalline Widmanstätten pattern, visible on cut surfaces, formed over millions of years of slow cooling at approximately 1°C per million years and cannot be replicated artificially. The bowl-shaped cavities on its surface were formed by terrestrial weathering after its arrival in Oregon, not by its passage through the atmosphere. Radioactive isotope analysis has been used to date the timing of its parent body's disruption.

## History & Controversy
The Clackamas people revered Tomanowos for centuries before European contact. They believed its cavities collected rainwater with healing and spiritual properties, used in ceremonies before hunting and warfare. When the meteorite was taken to New York, the Confederated Tribes of Grand Ronde launched a legal case for its return. A 2000 settlement allowed the tribes to conduct an annual ceremony at the American Museum of Natural History and required that any future transfer of the meteorite would require tribal consent. It is one of the most significant legal precedents for Indigenous cultural property rights in the United States.

## For Young Visitors
This giant lump of metal fell from space about a billion years ago — that's before dinosaurs even existed! It's so heavy that 200 of you standing together would weigh less than it does. It travelled all the way from Canada to Oregon inside a glacier during the Ice Age. Native American people considered it sacred and collected the rainwater from its holes for special ceremonies. It's literally a piece of another planet that crashed into ours.

## Conservation Status
The original meteorite is displayed in the Arthur Ross Hall of Meteorites at the American Museum of Natural History. Iron meteorites are susceptible to oxidation (rusting) in humid conditions. The AMNH maintains the meteorite at controlled humidity and applies a protective coating. Despite its hardness, the surface shows active conservation management.
