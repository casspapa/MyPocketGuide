## Overview
This monumental veduta painting by Giovanni Paolo Panini (1691-1765) depicts the Trevi Fountain in Rome shortly after its completion in 1762, capturing the fountain at the height of its Baroque grandeur and the social spectacle surrounding it. Panini was the foremost painter of Roman architectural views in the 18th century, and this work is among his finest surviving canvases.

## Scientific Significance
Panini's vedute (view paintings) are primary historical documents for the study of 18th century Rome. His depictions of architectural monuments show details of surface finish, colour, and surrounding urban fabric that have since been altered. X-ray fluorescence mapping of this canvas has identified his characteristic palette — lead white, smalt (blue glass pigment), vermilion, and yellow ochre — consistent with mid-18th century Roman workshop practice. The painting's perspective has been used by architectural historians to determine the original colour of the Trevi Fountain's travertine stone before atmospheric discolouration.

## History & Controversy
The Trevi Fountain was completed in 1762 after nearly 30 years of construction, just three years before Panini's death — he barely lived to paint the finished monument he had depicted in earlier stages. The fountain was built over the terminal point of the ancient Aqua Virgo aqueduct, which had supplied water to Rome since 19 BCE. The tradition of throwing a coin dates not from antiquity but from the 1954 film Three Coins in the Fountain. The fountain became a symbol of Rome's post-war glamour through Federico Fellini's 1960 film La Dolce Vita. In 2024, Rome introduced fines of up to €450 for dipping feet or hands in the fountain.

## For Young Visitors
In this painting you're looking at Rome's most famous fountain just after it was finished — it looked brand new! People have been throwing coins in the Trevi Fountain for decades because of a film from the 1950s that said if you throw a coin over your shoulder, you'll come back to Rome. Millions of tourists do it every year and the fountain collects about €1.5 million — all of which goes to help people who need food. The painter who made this picture was so famous for painting Rome that rich tourists visiting on their Grand Tour paid huge amounts to take his paintings home as souvenirs.

## Conservation Status
The painting is displayed under low UV lighting to prevent fading of light-sensitive pigments, particularly the smalt blue. The canvas has been relined (a new canvas attached to the back) to stabilise the original. Surface cleaning removed discoloured varnish layers in 2019, revealing significantly brighter colours than previously visible. The painting's condition is currently stable.
