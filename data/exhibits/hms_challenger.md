## Overview
The HMS Challenger expedition of 1872-1876 is considered the founding voyage of modern oceanography. A Royal Navy corvette converted into a scientific vessel, Challenger circumnavigated the globe, taking depth soundings, collecting water and biological samples, and measuring temperatures across all the world's major oceans. The Museum's collection includes original dredging equipment, specimen jars, and the scientific instruments used aboard.

## Key Facts
- Expedition dates: December 21, 1872 to May 24, 1876
- Total distance: 127,600 kilometres (68,890 nautical miles)
- Led by: Captain George Nares (naval) and scientist Charles Wyville Thomson
- Ship: HMS Challenger, a 69-metre steam corvette
- Crew: 243 officers, men and scientists
- Deepest sounding recorded: 8,185 metres in the Pacific — the Challenger Deep in the Mariana Trench is named after this expedition
- 4,700 new species discovered
- 492 depth soundings taken
- 133 dredge hauls
- Report: 50 volumes, 29,500 pages, took 19 years to publish (1880-1895)
- Challenger Deep (10,935 metres) named in honour of the expedition

## Scientific Significance
The Challenger expedition established that life exists throughout the ocean, including the deep sea — overturning the prevailing "azoic theory" that the deep ocean was lifeless. The 4,700 new species catalogued transformed marine biology. The systematic depth soundings for the first time revealed the topography of the ocean floor — mountains, trenches, and plains previously unknown. The discovery of manganese nodules on the seafloor has significance today as they contain cobalt, nickel, and rare earth elements that are targets for deep-sea mining operations.

## History & Controversy
The scientific report took 76 scientists 19 years to complete and ran to 50 volumes. Principal scientist Charles Wyville Thomson died before it was finished. The expedition's naturalist, John Murray, took over and completed the work. The Challenger Deep — the deepest known point on Earth at 10,935 metres in the Mariana Trench — is named for the expedition that first measured a depth in that region, though the exact deepest point was not measured until later. The modern era of oceanography traces its direct lineage to Challenger — NASA's space shuttle Challenger and the Apollo 17 lunar module were both named in honour of the ship.

## For Young Visitors
Before HMS Challenger set sail, most scientists thought the deep ocean was completely empty — too dark, too cold, too crushed for anything to live there. The Challenger proved them completely wrong by hauling up thousands of weird and wonderful creatures from the depths. The deepest part of the ocean — the Challenger Deep — is named after this ship. It's so deep that if you dropped Mount Everest in, the tip would still be underwater! The expedition's scientists found so many new species it took them 19 years just to write it all up.

## Conservation Status
Original Challenger artefacts are distributed across multiple institutions including the Natural History Museum London, the Scottish Oceanographic Society, and the National Maritime Museum. The specimen collection — preserved in Victorian-era fixatives — requires ongoing conservation. Many jars have been resealed and the preservative replaced. The original scientific instruments are displayed in controlled humidity to prevent corrosion.
