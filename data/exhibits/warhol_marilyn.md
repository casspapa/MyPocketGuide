Visual cues: Brightly coloured silkscreen print of Marilyn Monroe's face from a publicity photo. Flat areas of vivid colour (yellow hair, pink face, red lips) with deliberately misaligned ink layers creating a blurred effect. Square canvas format.

## Overview
This silkscreen print is from Andy Warhol's iconic Marilyn series, produced in 1962 immediately following Marilyn Monroe's death on August 4, 1962. Warhol used a publicity photograph from the 1953 film Niagara as his source image, applying his signature silkscreen technique with deliberately misregistered colour. The Marilyn series is among the most recognised works of 20th century art and a defining statement of Pop Art.

## Key Facts
- Artist: Andy Warhol (1928-1987)
- Date: 1962
- Medium: silkscreen ink on synthetic polymer paint on canvas
- Source photograph: Niagara (1953) promotional still, photographed by Gene Korman
- Warhol produced the series within weeks of Monroe's death on August 4, 1962
- The most famous work in the series, Shot Marilyns (1964), sold for $195 million in 2022 — the most expensive 20th century artwork ever sold at auction
- Warhol's Factory produced multiple versions in different colour combinations
- The series directly inspired the concept of the celebrity image as mass-produced commodity
- Warhol was 33 when he made the Marilyn series — it made his international reputation

## Scientific Significance
Warhol's silkscreen technique involves coating a screen with a light-sensitive emulsion, projecting a photographic image onto it, and using squeegees to force ink through the screen onto the canvas surface. The deliberate misregistration of colours — where the ink doesn't perfectly align with the underlying image — creates the characteristic blurring effect. Technical analysis using multispectral imaging has shown Warhol frequently worked in series by varying the colour relationships systematically. The work raised fundamental questions in aesthetics about the distinction between original and reproduction that remain central to contemporary art theory.

## History & Controversy
The Marilyn series was produced in the immediate aftermath of Monroe's death — Warhol began work within weeks. Critics at the time were divided: some saw the works as a profound meditation on celebrity, death, and the dehumanising effect of mass media; others as crass commercial exploitation of a tragedy. Warhol's response was characteristically oblique. The Shot Marilyns (1964) series has its own remarkable provenance — a woman named Dorothy Podber walked into Warhol's Factory and, given permission to "shoot" the paintings, pulled out a pistol and fired through four stacked canvases. Rather than destroying them, Warhol repaired and sold them — the bullet holes are documented in conservation records. The 2022 sale of Shot Sage Blue Marilyn for $195 million was criticised by some as obscene given global inequality; defended by others as confirming Warhol's prescient understanding of the art market as spectacle.

## For Young Visitors
Andy Warhol made this painting right after Marilyn Monroe died, using the same technique as printing a T-shirt. He was interested in the idea that famous people become like products — their faces get repeated over and over until they stop being a real person and become just an image. The most famous version of this painting sold for $195 million — more than most countries' annual budgets for arts funding. One time someone literally shot four of these paintings with a real gun, and instead of throwing them away, Warhol just repaired them and sold them for even more money.

## Conservation Status
Silkscreen works on synthetic polymer are susceptible to paint layer cracking and surface dirt accumulation in the ink layers. Warhol's works have been extensively studied by the Andy Warhol Foundation for the Visual Arts, which maintains a catalogue raisonné. This work is displayed under controlled lighting with UV filtering. The synthetic polymer paint used by Warhol in the early 1960s has shown long-term stability issues that are an active area of contemporary conservation research.
