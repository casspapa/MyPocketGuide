## Visual Identification
Keywords: marble statue, female figure, deer, hunting pose, quiver, arrows, Roman sculpture, Greek goddess
Commonly called: "Diana of Versailles", "Artemis", "Diana the Huntress", "the hunting goddess", "Diana with a Doe", "Diane chasseresse"
Visual cues: Slightly over-lifesize marble statue (2.01 metres) of a woman in motion, striding forward as if pursuing game. Right arm raised reaching for an arrow from a quiver on her back. A small deer stands at her feet. She wears a short Dorian chiton with a himation around her waist and sandals. Classical Greek style with flowing drapery suggesting rapid movement.

## Overview
The Diana of Versailles is a Roman marble copy (1st or 2nd century CE) of a lost Greek bronze original attributed to the sculptor Leochares, dating to approximately 325 BCE. It depicts Diana (Greek: Artemis), goddess of the hunt, in dynamic forward motion with a deer at her side. The statue has been one of France's most treasured artworks for nearly five centuries, passing through the hands of popes, kings, and emperors before finding its permanent home in the Louvre.

## Key Facts
- Artist: Roman copyist, after a Greek bronze original attributed to Leochares (c. 325 BCE)
- Date: 1st or 2nd century CE (Roman copy)
- Medium: marble
- Height: 2.01 metres
- Current location: Musée du Louvre, Paris
- Given by Pope Paul IV to King Henry II of France in 1556
- The gift carried a deliberate allusion to the king's famous mistress, Diane de Poitiers, whose name echoed the goddess
- Originally installed as the centrepiece of the Jardin de la Reine at the Château de Fontainebleau — one of the first major Roman sculptures displayed in France
- Moved to the Palais du Louvre in 1602 by Henri IV, installed in a gallery specially designed for it (the Salle des Antiques, now the Salle des Caryatides)
- Displayed in the Hall of Mirrors at the Palace of Versailles by Louis XIV in 1696
- Returned to the Louvre in 1798 during the French Revolution
- The left arm and the deer are later restorations — the original may have held a bow, and may have been accompanied by a hunting dog rather than a deer
- Her second toes are longer than her big toes, a condition known as Morton's toe, which the ancient Greeks considered a mark of beauty
- A miniature replica was aboard the RMS Titanic in 1912 and was rediscovered in the wreckage debris field in 1986
- Comparable Roman copies of the same original have been found at Leptis Magna (Libya), Antalya (Turkey), and Annaba (Algeria)

## Scientific Significance
The statue is attributed to Leochares, a Greek sculptor active in the mid-4th century BCE who also worked on the Mausoleum at Halicarnassus, one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World. The original bronze would have been created using the lost-wax casting technique. The Roman marble copy demonstrates the Roman practice of reproducing Greek masterworks — the copyist had to solve the engineering challenge of supporting a dynamic striding pose in heavy marble that the original bronze handled through its own tensile strength, which is why the deer was likely added as a structural support for the composition. The flowing drapery of the short chiton is carved to suggest rapid motion, with fabric pressing against the body in front and billowing behind — a technique perfected in the late Classical period to convey movement in static stone. Analysis of the marble surface has revealed traces of ancient polychromy, indicating the statue was originally painted in lifelike colours.

## History & Controversy
The Diana of Versailles has a remarkably well-documented provenance spanning nearly five centuries of French royal collections. Pope Paul IV's gift to Henry II was loaded with political and personal subtext — the king's powerful mistress Diane de Poitiers had adopted the imagery of Diana the huntress as her personal emblem, making the papal gift simultaneously diplomatic and provocative. The statue became one of the earliest Roman sculptures prominently displayed in France and acquired a reputation across Europe rivalling the masterpieces of the Vatican Belvedere collection. Its journey through Fontainebleau, the Louvre, Versailles, and back to the Louvre mirrors the shifting centres of French royal power. Multiple bronze replicas were cast over the centuries: Barthélemy Prieur made one in 1605, the Keller brothers in 1684, and Hubert Le Sueur created a version for Charles I of England in 1634. By the late 18th century, the Diana had become so iconic that reproductions in bronze, plaster, and lead proliferated across Europe. Napoleon himself redistributed the various copies, gifting Prieur's bronze to Empress Joséphine for Malmaison.
