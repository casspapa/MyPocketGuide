## Overview
The Antikythera Mechanism is an ancient Greek analogue computer — the oldest known complex geared mechanism in the world. Recovered from a shipwreck off the island of Antikythera in 1901, it was used to predict astronomical positions, eclipses, and the timing of the ancient Olympic Games. It is approximately 2,100 years old and was not surpassed in mechanical complexity until the astronomical clocks of 14th century Europe.

## Key Facts
- Constructed approximately 100 BCE, possibly as early as 200 BCE
- Recovered from a Roman-era shipwreck in 60 metres of water in 1901
- Contains at least 30 interlocking bronze gears
- Could predict solar and lunar eclipses using the Saros cycle
- Tracked the positions of the five planets known to ancient Greeks
- Displayed the Metonic cycle (235 lunar months = 19 solar years)
- Had a dial tracking the four-year cycle of the Panhellenic Games including the Olympics
- Inscriptions on the mechanism are in ancient Greek Corinthian dialect — suggesting manufacture in Corinth or a colony
- Original object was approximately the size of a shoebox
- The Museum holds a precision bronze replica; the fragments are in Athens

## Scientific Significance
The mechanism demonstrates that ancient Greek technology was far more sophisticated than previously understood. For over 50 years after its discovery it was misidentified as an astrolabe. It was not until 1951 that historian Derek de Solla Price began the systematic analysis that revealed its true nature. Modern CT scanning and X-ray imaging in the 2000s revealed internal gearing and inscriptions invisible to the naked eye. The 2021 UCL Antikythera Research Team produced the first complete theoretical model of all the mechanism's functions, resolving debates about its front and back dials that had persisted for decades.

## History & Controversy
The shipwreck that carried the mechanism was discovered by sponge divers sheltering from a storm. Along with the mechanism, the wreck yielded bronze and marble statues, glassware, and jewellery — one of the richest ancient shipwreck discoveries ever made. The mechanism's fragments sat largely unexamined in the Athens National Archaeological Museum for decades. The question of who built it remains debated — the island of Rhodes, whose mathematician Hipparchus developed the astronomical theories the device employs, is the leading candidate. A second expedition to the wreck site in 2017 using ROVs recovered additional human remains and artefacts.

## For Young Visitors
Imagine building a calculator out of tiny metal gears — 2,100 years ago, before electricity, before computers, before even clocks! That's what someone in ancient Greece did. This machine could tell you when the next eclipse would happen and when the Olympic Games were coming up. Scientists ignored it for 50 years before realising what it actually was. It's basically the world's first computer, and it was lost at the bottom of the sea for 2,000 years.

## Conservation Status
The 82 surviving fragments are housed in the National Archaeological Museum in Athens under strictly controlled temperature and humidity. The bronze has developed a stable patina that protects the underlying metal. Further CT scanning campaigns are planned to extract remaining legible text from encrusted surfaces.
