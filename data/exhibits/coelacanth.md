## Overview
The coelacanth is one of the greatest zoological discoveries of the 20th century — a fish scientists believed had been extinct for 66 million years, until a living specimen was pulled from the waters off South Africa in 1938. The Museum's specimen is a preserved example of Latimeria chalumnae, the West Indian Ocean coelacanth.

## Key Facts
- Fossil record dates back 400 million years — older than the dinosaurs
- Believed extinct since the end-Cretaceous mass extinction 66 million years ago
- First living specimen caught December 22, 1938 off East London, South Africa
- Identified by Marjorie Courtenay-Latimer, a museum curator, from a fishing trawl
- Can reach 2 metres in length and weigh up to 90kg
- Lives at depths of 150-700 metres in submarine caves
- Lobe-finned fish — fins attached to fleshy, limb-like structures
- Second species (Latimeria menadoensis) discovered in Indonesia in 1999
- Gives birth to live young — gestation period estimated at 3 years

## Scientific Significance
The coelacanth's lobe fins are anatomically similar to the limbs of early tetrapods — the four-limbed vertebrates that first crawled onto land. For this reason coelacanths are intensely studied as a living window into the evolutionary transition from water to land. Their genome, sequenced in 2013, shows they have evolved remarkably slowly compared to other vertebrates — earning the description "living fossil," though scientists prefer "lazily evolving."

## History & Controversy
The 1938 discovery by Marjorie Courtenay-Latimer was initially dismissed by the scientific establishment. She preserved the fish as best she could and sent a sketch to ichthyologist J.L.B. Smith, who confirmed the identification but missed the original specimen due to the Christmas holiday. It took 14 more years before a second specimen was caught. The discovery made global headlines and is considered one of the most important zoological finds of the 20th century. Courtenay-Latimer received little recognition for decades — the species was named after her only posthumously in broader acknowledgement.

## For Young Visitors
Imagine finding an animal that scientists thought disappeared with the dinosaurs — that's exactly what happened with the coelacanth! It was hiding at the bottom of the ocean for millions of years without anyone knowing. It also has fins that look almost like little arms, which scientists think might be what the first animals to walk on land looked like. It's basically a real-life living fossil!

## Conservation Status
Both coelacanth species are listed as Critically Endangered on the IUCN Red List. The primary threats are accidental capture in deep-water fishing nets (bycatch) and habitat destruction. Estimated population of Latimeria chalumnae is fewer than 500 individuals. They are strictly protected under CITES Appendix I.
