Visual cues: Conical spacecraft capsule approximately 3.9 metres tall and wide. Scorched and ablated heat shield on the base. Metallic silver/grey body with visible hatches and windows. Full-scale replica.

## Overview
Columbia is the Command Module that carried Neil Armstrong, Buzz Aldrin, and Michael Collins to the Moon and back in July 1969. It is the most visited object in the history of the National Air and Space Museum in Washington DC. The Museum displays a full-scale precision replica alongside mission artefacts; the original Columbia is in Washington. Apollo 11 remains the only mission in human history to land humans on another world.

## Key Facts
- Launch: July 16, 1969 from Kennedy Space Center, Florida
- Moon landing: July 20, 1969, 20:17 UTC — Sea of Tranquility
- First words on the Moon: "The Eagle has landed" (Armstrong)
- First step on lunar surface: July 21, 1969, 02:56 UTC
- Total mission duration: 8 days, 3 hours, 18 minutes, 35 seconds
- Distance travelled: approximately 1,530,000 km
- Command Module dimensions: 3.9 metres tall, 3.9 metres wide at base
- Crew: Neil Armstrong (Commander), Buzz Aldrin (Lunar Module Pilot), Michael Collins (Command Module Pilot)
- Collins orbited the Moon alone in Columbia while Armstrong and Aldrin walked on the surface
- Re-entry speed: approximately 40,000 km/h
- Heat shield temperature on re-entry: approximately 2,800°C
- 382kg of lunar rock and soil samples returned

## Scientific Significance
Apollo 11 transformed planetary science. The 382kg of lunar samples returned proved the Moon formed from debris ejected when a Mars-sized body struck the early Earth approximately 4.5 billion years ago — the Giant Impact Hypothesis. The samples contained no water and no life, resolving a major debate. The retroreflector arrays placed on the lunar surface by Armstrong and Aldrin are still used today — laser ranging experiments continue to measure the Moon's recession from Earth (3.8cm per year) with millimetre precision.

## History & Controversy
The decision to go to the Moon was explicitly political — President Kennedy's 1961 declaration was a Cold War response to Soviet space achievements. The Apollo programme cost approximately $25.4 billion (roughly $180 billion in 2024 dollars). Whether the Moon landings were "worth it" economically remains debated. The conspiracy theory that the landings were faked — first widely promoted in a 1976 book — has been comprehensively debunked by independent evidence from multiple countries including the Soviet Union, which tracked the mission in real time. Michael Collins, who orbited alone, is often called the forgotten astronaut — he was 111km from the nearest human beings when at the far side of the Moon.

## For Young Visitors
Three astronauts flew to the Moon in a spacecraft not much bigger than a small car! Two of them actually walked on the Moon while one stayed behind orbiting all by himself. They were so far from Earth that when Michael Collins went around the far side of the Moon, he was more alone than any human being has ever been — completely out of contact with everyone. They brought back rocks from the Moon that scientists are still studying today. And the whole trip happened before smartphones, before the internet, before most of the technology you use every day.

## Conservation Status
The original Columbia shows significant scorching and ablation on its heat shield from re-entry. It has not been cleaned or restored — the conservation philosophy is to preserve it exactly as it returned from the Moon. The interior shows 50 years of careful preservation. The craft is displayed in a climate-controlled case at the National Air and Space Museum.
